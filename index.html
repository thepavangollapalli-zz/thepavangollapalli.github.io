Hello!

I love to create and collaborate. Activities such as hackathons, research, and DECA have helped me discover the beautiful intricacy of relying on myself and working with others to render imagination into reality.

When I'm not working, however, I love playing tennis and ultimate frisbee while avidly keeping up with the latest fútbol news. I read as well; no book is safe from me. I've just finished the Lord of the Rings trilogy and the Anne of Green Gables series, and I plan on starting on Dickens or whatever dystopian teen fiction Hunger Games knockoff comes out next.

I'll be attending Carnegie Mellon University next fall, majoring in Information Systems and Computer Science, blowing bagpipes, and continuing to create.

GalaxyDefenders

May 2015

Java, GridWorld, JUnit Testing

Using Gridworld and Java, I and two partners made a version of Space Invaders for our APCS final project. The game maintained a version of the original gameplay while adding a powered bullet that passed through all the enemies in its column. We also completed a specification sheet containing a class structure and javadocs for all associated classes and methods. In addition to working on modifying Gridworld to create the game, I used JUnit to write a testing class that tested both the functions of individual methods and complex actions the game executed.

Freakency

January 2015

HTML, CSS, Javascript, Android

At the Hacking Generation Y hackathon, my team and I created a silly party game that broadcast a high-pitched sound from a random phone among a bunch of phones and asked people to guess at the source of the sound. The phones would communicate among each other to pick a random phone and switch the sources during the course of the game, so people would have more of a challenge. Using Twilio and PubNub's APIs, we created this app in about a 12 hour stretch of coding. I helped to build the initial layout of the app, test it, and put the presentation website together.

face-ratios

June-August 2014

Java, Python, OpenCV

Working at Rice University's EECS department, I wanted to do something linking computer vision to people. After much brainstorming, I decided to scan the faces of attractive people, calculate the ratios of certain distances in their facial structure (such as interpupillary differences or the length of the nose), and map the results to the background of the people. This was my first research project, and a lot of it was spent trying to figure out the setup of OpenCV and its assorted dependencies on my computer (I had a brief, unsuccessful struggle with MATLAB before switching to Python and then Java).
